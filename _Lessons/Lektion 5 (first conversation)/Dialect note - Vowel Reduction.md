### Vowels are not always pronounced fully in natural speech
In the previous lesson we said that Arabic vowels are pronounced clearly and consistently:
**aa – uu – ii**
This is true in principle, but in natural speech vowels in many languages often become **softer or slightly different**. This also happens in Syrian Arabic.
Before looking at Arabic examples, we can observe the same thing in **German and English**.

---
### Examples from German
In German, vowels in unstressed syllables are often **weakened or neutralized**.
Examples:
- **haben** → the **e** is not a full _e_, but a weak sound: _ha-ben_
- **bitte** → the last **e** is very soft: _bit-te_
- **interessant** → often pronounced closer to **intressant**

The vowels are still there, but they are **not pronounced as clearly**.

---
### Examples from English
English shows this even more strongly.
Examples:
- **complete** → sounds closer to **cmplete**
- **interesting** → often pronounced **intresting**
- **family** → often pronounced **famly**

Many vowels become very weak or almost disappear.

---
### The linguistic concept
This phenomenon is called **vowel reduction**.
It means that **vowels become weaker or more neutral in natural speech**, especially in unstressed syllables.

---
### Examples from Syrian Arabic
A similar effect can happen in the Syrian dialect.
#### Long vowels
Sometimes long vowels shift slightly:
- **uu (و)** can sound closer to **o**
    - **كول (kūl)** → often pronounced **kol**
- **ii (ي)** can sound closer to **e**
    - **ليش (lēsh)** → often pronounced **lesh**
#### Short vowels
Short vowels can also be softened.
Example:
- **شِبَك؟ (shibak?)**  
    The **i** often sounds softer, closer to **e**: **shebak**

---
### Important idea
The vowel system is still the same (**aa, uu, ii and short vowels**), but in natural speech vowels can sometimes become **softer or slightly different**.
So if you hear a pronunciation that sounds a bit different, it may simply be **vowel reduction**.
