**Definiteness** is the property of a noun or noun phrase that indicates whether it refers to a **specific, identifiable entity** or to **an unspecified member of a category**.

A **definite** noun phrase refers to something that the speaker expects the listener to be able to identify, either because it has already been mentioned, because it is unique, or because the context makes it clear.

Examples:
- **das Buch** (*the book*)
- **der Lehrer** (*the teacher*)
- **Berlin**
- **Deutschland**
- **Anna**

In all of these cases, the speaker is referring to one specific entity.

An **indefinite** noun phrase, on the other hand, refers to an unspecified member of a category.

Examples:
- **ein Buch** (*a book*)
- **ein Lehrer** (*a teacher*)
- **eine Schule** (*a school*)
- **ein Hund** (*a dog*)

Here, the speaker is not referring to any particular object or person, but simply to one example of that category.

Compare:

- **Ich lese ein Buch.**  
  *I am reading a book.* (The listener does not know which book.)

- **Ich lese das Buch.**  
  *I am reading the book.* (The listener can identify which book is meant.)

In German, definiteness is usually expressed through **articles**:

- **Definite articles:** **der, die, das**
- **Indefinite articles:** **ein, eine, ein**

However, not all nouns require an article to be definite. Names such as **Anna**, **Berlin**, and **Deutschland** are already understood to refer to unique entities and are therefore inherently definite.

In summary, **definiteness describes whether a noun phrase refers to a specific, identifiable entity or to an unspecified member of a category**.
