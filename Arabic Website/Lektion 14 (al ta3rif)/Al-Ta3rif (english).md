# The Arabic Definite Article (الـ)

In **Syrian Arabic**, the **definite article** is **al-**, which corresponds to **der, die, das** in German.

It is attached directly to the beginning of a noun to make it **definite**.

Examples:

- **ktāb** — *ein Buch*
- **əl-ktāb** — *das Buch*

- **bēt** — *ein Haus*
- **əl-bēt** — *das Haus*

## Pronunciation

Although the article is written **al-**, its pronunciation varies depending on the sounds around it.

In everyday Syrian Arabic, it is commonly pronounced as **l-**, **el-**, or **le-**, depending on the sentence and the following word.

### Sun Letters
![[sun letters]]

When **al-** comes before a word beginning with a **[[sun letters|sun letter]]**, the **l** sound disappears and the first consonant of the noun is **doubled**. In this case, the article is effectively pronounced as **e-**.

For example:
- **esh-shams** (*die Sonne*), not *el-shams*.

# The Indefinite Article

Unlike German, **Arabic has no indefinite article** corresponding to **ein** or **eine**.

Instead, a common noun is **indefinite by default** if it does **not** have **al-** attached to it.

This gives a simple rule:

- **Common noun + al- → Definite**
- **Common noun without al- → Indefinite**

For example:

| Arabic (transliteration) | German |
|--------------------------|--------|
| **fəndoʾ** | *ein Hotel* |
| **əl-fəndoʾ** | *das Hotel* |
| **bēt** | *ein Haus* |
| **əl-bēt** | *das Haus* |

In other words, if an ordinary noun appears **without** **al-**, it should usually be translated using **ein** or **eine** in German.
