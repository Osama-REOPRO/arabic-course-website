# Definiteness in Arabic

The concepts of **common nouns**, **proper nouns**, and **definiteness** also exist in Arabic.

Just like in German and English, **proper nouns** refer to unique entities and are therefore usually **inherently definite**. They normally do not require an article.

Examples:

- **أحمد** (_Ahmad_)
- **دمشق** (_Damascus_)
- **سوريا** (_Syria_)

These names are already definite because they identify a specific person or place.

**Common nouns**, on the other hand, refer to general categories. They can be either **definite** or **indefinite**, and Arabic distinguishes between these two forms using the **definite article** **الـ** (_al-_).

Examples:

- **كتاب** (_a book_)
    
- **الكتاب** (_the book_)
    
- **بيت** (_a house_)
    
- **البيت** (_the house_)
    

Unlike English and German, Arabic has **only a definite article**. There is **no indefinite article** corresponding to English _a/an_ or German _ein/eine_.

Instead, a common noun is understood to be **indefinite by default** if it does **not** have the prefix **الـ** (_al-_). Adding **الـ** makes the noun definite.

This leads to a very simple rule:

- **Common noun + الـ → Definite**
    
- **Common noun without الـ → Indefinite**
    
- **Proper noun → Usually definite without any article**
    

For example:

|Arabic|Meaning|
|---|---|
|**كتاب**|_a book_|
|**الكتاب**|_the book_|
|**أحمد**|_Ahmad_ (already definite)|
|**دمشق**|_Damascus_ (already definite)|

In other words, Arabic expresses definiteness primarily by adding **الـ** to common nouns, while the absence of **الـ** generally indicates that a common noun is indefinite.
