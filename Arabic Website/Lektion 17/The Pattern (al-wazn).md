# The Pattern (al-wazn)

## Spot the Similarity

Let's look at five words from five completely different roots:

- maktab (k-t-b) — office
- maTba5 (T-b-5) — kitchen
- mashghal (sh-gh-l) — workshop
- madraset (d-r-s) — school
- maghsale (gh-s-l) — laundry room / sink

What do you notice? The roots are all different — k-t-b, T-b-5, sh-gh-l, d-r-s, gh-s-l — and yet something about these words sounds the same.

Look closely at the shape of each word: **ma + [root] + (e)**. An **m** at the front, the three root letters in the middle, and that's basically it.

---

## A Second One

Let's look at another set of words, again from different roots:

- Tabba5 (T-b-5) — cook / chef
- 7alla2 (7-l-2) — barber
- sawwa2 (s-w-2) — driver
- 5ayyaT (5-y-T) — tailor
- bayyaa3 (b-y-3) — salesman

Same question: the roots are all different — T-b-5, 7-l-2, s-w-2, 5-y-T, b-y-3 — but what do you notice about the shape of these words?

Look closely: **[root1] + a + [root2][root2] + a + [root3]**. The middle root letter is doubled, and the vowels around it are always the same. Every one of these words describes a **person who does this as their profession or habitually**.

---

## A Third Pattern

Let's look at one more set of words — this time using roots you already know:

- katab (k-t-b) — he wrote
- daras (d-r-s) — he studied
- fata7 (f-t-7) — he opened
- ghasal (gh-s-l) — he washed
- 9ara2 (9-r-2) — he read

Same question as before: the roots are all different, but what do you notice about the shape of these words?

Look closely: **[root1] a [root2] a [root3]**. Every single one of these words means "**he did X**" — he wrote, he studied, he opened, he washed, he read. This is the most basic verb shape in Arabic, and you'll see it constantly.

---

## The Rule: Patterns (al-wazn)

You've just discovered the second half of the Arabic word-building system. While the **root** carries the *meaning* of a word, the **pattern** — called **al-wazn** (literally "the weight" or "the measure") — carries the *role* of a word. It's the mould that the root gets poured into.

> **Root** = what it's about
> **Pattern** = what kind of word it is

You can think of a pattern as a kind of **function**, like in math: you feed a root in, and a finished word comes out the other end — one that combines the meaning of the root with the role of the pattern.

> Input: the idea of "writing" (k-t-b) → fed into the **place pattern** (maf3al) → Output: a word for *a place where writing happens* — "office" or "library."

Look at what we just saw:

| Pattern | Shape | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| ma-[root]-(e) | maktab, maTba5, mashghal, madraset, maghsale | a *place* where the root-action happens |
| [r1]a[r2][r2]a[r3] | Tabba5, 7alla2, sawwa2, 5ayyaT, bayyaa3 | a *person* who does the root-action professionally |
| [r1]a[r2]a[r3] | katab, daras, fata7, ghasal, 9ara2 | "**he did**" the root-action (past tense) |

Writing patterns like **[r1]a[r2][r2]a[r3]** every time is clunky and hard to remember. So Arabic grammarians came up with a clever shortcut: they use one specific placeholder root — **ف-ع-ل (f-3-l)** — as a stand-in for "any root." Instead of writing [r1]a[r2][r2]a[r3], you simply write **fa33al**, where f, 3, and l mark the position of root letter 1, 2, and 3.

So instead of the awkward bracket notation, the three patterns above are simply written as:

| Pattern name | Shape (with placeholder) | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| maf3al | ma-f-3-a-l | a *place* where the action happens |
| fa33aal | fa-3-3-aa-l | a *person* who does it professionally |
| fa3al | fa-3-a-l | "**he did**" (past tense) |

This is the real power of Arabic vocabulary: once you know a root (the meaning) and a pattern (the role), you can often **build or guess words you've never heard before**.

## Mapping Roots onto f-3-l

| Root | f (1st letter) | 3 (2nd letter) | l (3rd letter) |
|---|---|---|---|
| k-t-b | k | t | b |
| T-b-5 | T | b | 5 |
| sh-gh-l | sh | gh | l |
| d-r-s | d | r | s |
| f-t-7 | f | t | 7 |
| gh-s-l | gh | s | l |
| 9-r-2 | 9 | r | 2 |

So when you see a pattern written as **fa3al**, you now know exactly what to do: take the first letter of your root and put it where the **f** is, the second letter where the **3** is, and the third letter where the **l** is.

---

## A Musical Way to Think About Patterns

Here's a useful way to think about what a pattern really is: imagine a melody — a specific rhythm and pitch contour — like the tune of a familiar song. You can hum that same melody using completely different lyrics, or even just "la la la," and it's still recognizably *the same tune*.

A pattern like **fa33aal** works the same way. It has its own "melody" — a rhythm of syllables and a specific vowel sound — completely independent of which root you sing it with. Once your ear knows the *tune* of fa33aal, you can take almost any root and "sing" it to that same tune, and out comes a real word.

By the end of this lesson, the goal isn't to consciously calculate "f equals this letter, 3 equals that letter." The goal is for your ear to recognize the *tune* of a pattern — and for inserting a root into it to become instinctive, the way humming a familiar melody with new words feels natural almost immediately.

## Exercise 1 — Same Tune, Different Roots

Let's take one pattern — **maf3al** (the "place" mould) — and run a few different roots through it. Watch how the same "melody" produces a different word each time, but the meaning always stays "a place where ___ happens."

| Root | Meaning of root | maf3al → Result | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| l-3-b (ل-ع-ب) | to play | mal3ab | playground / stadium |
| k-t-b (ك-ت-ب) | to write | maktab | office |
| 3-m-l (ع-م-ل) | to do / work | ma3mal | factory |
| s-b-7 (س-ب-ح) | to swim | masba7 | swimming pool |
| 5-r-j (خ-ر-ج) | to exit | ma5raj | exit |
| w-9-f (و-ق-ف) | to stop / park | maw9af | parking spot / station |
| 3-r-D (ع-ر-ض) | to display | ma3raD | exhibition |

Notice: same rhythm, same vowel pattern, every time — just a new root slotted in, and the meaning shifts to match the new root while the "place" feeling stays constant.

> [!NOTE] Note
> Although maw9af is the "textbook" form, in everyday Damascus speech it's almost always pronounced **maw9ef**.

Now let's flip it around: take a single root and run it through different patterns. Watch how the *meaning* of the root stays the same, but the *role* of the word completely changes depending on which pattern it's sung to.

## Wrap-Up

This is the instinct we're aiming for: once your ear recognizes the *tune* of a pattern — fa3al, maf3al, fa33aal, whatever it may be — you'll be able to take almost any root and sing it to that tune without even thinking about it. The root gives you the meaning, the pattern gives you the role, and together they hand you a real, usable word — even one you've never heard before.

> [!NOTE] Important
> Not every pattern fits every root. Each root naturally fits into some patterns, while others would produce a meaningless result or simply aren't used that way in real speech. So you can't just plug any root into any pattern and assume the word exists — you still need to check (or get a feel through exposure) for which patterns a given root actually appears in.

> [!NOTE] Why This Matters
> This is also exactly what makes Arabic vocabulary so efficient to build. Learn one root, and you don't just learn one word — you learn the *seed* for several words at once. From there, all you need to pick up is which patterns that particular root happens to fit into. That's a much faster way to grow your vocabulary than memorizing every word as a completely separate, unconnected item.
